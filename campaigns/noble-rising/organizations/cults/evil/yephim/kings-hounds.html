The King's Hounds

Description

The King's Hounds is a cult worshiping the nessian warhound (hell hound) Yephim, who seeks the dominance of the forces of Hell and the return of the Red King. The King's Hounds nip at the heels of the institutions that try to enforce goodness and those that try to prevent the return of the Red King.

The King's Hounds form small, well organized warbands that are typically lead by either a Howler (cleric) or a chieftain, which then breaks down into squads of 4 to 12 soldiers or conscripts commanded by a sergeant, who is always a trusted servant of the warband's leader. If a warband is successful enough for long enough, they may eventually form a tribe of anywhere from 100 to 600 members, with another 40% being noncombatants in the form of slaves and children.

Tenants

  • The Grand Mission comes first, then the clan, then oneself.
  • Wasting subordinates is wasting the resources of Yephim himself.
  • A member may never eat the meat of a dog.

Relations

The King's Hounds typically operate fast enough that they don't have time to interact with other cults, except those that oppose them. The Hounds don't like the Calmers of the Flame Lord or the Servants of Perfect Beauty, because both lack a true, driving objective.

While the Hounds don't have any regular contact with the Liberation Guild, they appreciate the idea of killing Sithrak. It doesn't hurt that killing Sithrak would greatly further the Hounds' goals.

The Hounds view the Fallenfeather Sisterhood with a mix of awe and disgust. They value the Sisterhood's efficient organization, the individual member's skill and their objective of liberation from Hell. However, the Hounds find Akkus's lack of a big picture goal and refusal to join the forces of Hell highly offensive.

Yephim, the Puppy of Doom

Yephim is a nessian warhound (hell hound) who seeks the dominance of the forces of Hell and of the Red King. Yephim uses his cult to wear down the will of the institutions that try to enforce goodness and that prevent the King's return.

Yephim teaches his servants that power comes as much from sudden violence as careful, deliberate, and slow action.
